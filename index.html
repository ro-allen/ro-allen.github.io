I am a PhD candidate at the University of Otago Department of Botany, supervised by Dr. Linn Hoffmann and Dr. Tina Summerfield. Previously, I completed an M.Res. in marine biology at Plymouth University supervised by Prof. Jason Hall-Spencer and Dr. Andy Foggo. My research interests centre around the fundamental dynamics of the marine microbiome, whilst my PhD thesis focusses on the use of high-throughput amplicon sequencing to investigate the community level responses of marine protists and bacteria to climate change. I am particularly interested in the handling of high-throughput sequencing data.

 

Current Projects

Microbial community assembly in the oligotrophic ocean

The patterns and processes involved in microbial community assembly in the marine environment are not well understood, particularly in the oligotrophic open ocean where data on the structure and distribution of microbial communites are scarce. During the ~2000 km GEOTRACES transect of the South Pacific Gyre aboard the RV Tangaroa, we sampled seawater from 24 locations at 4 depths to determine microbial community structure (16S amplicon sequencing) alongiside a suite of environmental parameters in order to investigate the drivers of microbial community assembly in these regions. These high-resolution data will act to inform fundamental mechanisms underpinning the distribution of microbes in the oligotrophic ocean, and have implications for the region in a changing environment.

 

Bacterioplankton responses to climate change in the oligotrophic ocean

Oligotrophic oceans represent ~40% of total ocean surface area, and account ~20% of marine primary production. Bacterioplankton are responsible for the majority of photosynthesis, nitrogen fixation, and recycling of organic material in these regions. The productivity of the oligotrophic ocean is closely coupled with the efficiency of organic matter recycling by heterotrophic bacteria as this process largely controls nutrient availability, due to limited inputs from vertical transport and low rates of nitrogen fixation. Subsequently, the responses of oligotrophic ocean bacterioplankton communities to ocean acidification and warming are likely to influence overall marine productivity and the global carbon cycle. During the GEOTRACES transect through the oligotrophic ocean aboard the RV Tangaroa, a team of scientists from NIWA and the University of Otago conducted a series of trace-metal clean deckboard incubation experiments to generate early data on this topic.

 

Eukaryotic phytoplankton responses to ocean acidification and warming in New Zealand coastal waters

The phytoplankton are a phylogenetically diverse group of autotrophic organisms, grouped together as a result of their functional similarity and shared occupancy of the photic ocean. Approximately 30% of global primary production can be attributed to eukaryotic phytoplankton, and these organisms underpin marine foodwebs. As part of New Zealand’s interdisciplinary CARIM mesocosm project, we aim characterise eukaryotic phytoplankton community responses to ocean acidification and warming and identify trends in genetic diversity within specific taxonomic groups using 18S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing and qPCR.

 

The impact of ocean acidification on marine biofilms: a CO2 seep study

Marine biolfilms are heterogenous microhabitats dominated by protists and bacteria. Marine biofilms provide a fundamental grazing resource for larger organisms such as gastropods in shallow marine ecosystems, whilst bacteria in biofilms play a further key role by cuing settlement in scleractinian corals and other habitat forming organisms. Understanding the effects of ocean acidification on biofilm community composition and succession from both a eukaryotic and prokaryotic perspective is critical, as potential shifts will have ramifications for the settlement of habitat forming organisms, trophic energy transfer in shallow marine ecosystems, and biodiversity directly associated with biofilms. High-throughput amplicon sequencing has opened the door to high-resolution profiling of marine biofilms. Here, we aim to characterise the effect of ocean acidification on biofilm community composition and succession by using volcanic CO2 seeps as a proxy of future acidified conditions.

 

Oceanic fronts: a tool for investigating phytoplankton-bacteria interactions

Oceanic fronts are dynamic regions where distinct water masses converge. These regions provide an opportunity to investigate environmental modulation of phytoplankton-bacteria interactions. Using high spatial resolution sampling of the Munida transect, we aim to construct a region specific phytoplankton-bacteria interaction network and elucidate the influence of environmental conditions on these interactions.
